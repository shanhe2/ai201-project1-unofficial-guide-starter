=== comment ===
For the CCI resources:
You will probably want to go through your advisor. They should fill out the form to create a project, then you need to file forms to become a CCI user.
As an RPI student you will get some free time on our IBM BlueGene/Q super computer. Normally, people pay to use these resources. The BlueGene/Q can run C, C++, and Fortran programs. See https://secure.cci.rpi.edu/wiki/index.php/Blue_Gene/Q for details about what libraries are available.
You should take Professor Carothers's Parallel Programming/Parallel Computing course in the Fall. He is the director of the CCI and many of the class projects involve working on the BlueGene.

=== comment ===
Note that the CCI resources use a 'batch' queuing system to run jobs. You log in to the front end compute nodes to compile your program. Then you use 'sbatch' or 'salloc' to run a script. The system waits until the requested resources are available, then it will run your job.

=== comment ===
Keep in mind that a supercomputer is really only useful for parallel programs, and generally not what your "folks that are just looking for massive RAM/processing power" know how to utilize.
Blue Gene Q has 1.6 GHz cores (slower than your laptop) with 1 GB of RAM per core (less than your laptop), so unless you can take advantage of a large number of cores, you'll likely be better off with your own computer.

=== comment ===
RPI has a bunch of supercomputer clusters. Since you posted the CCI wiki, I assume you're talking about the BlueGene Q, but there are clusters in biotech and CII that could also potentially be useful for you. I can tell you that access is dependent on you either being part of a class that uses it, or working under a professor for a project. Limitations are imposed by a scheduler that takes into account the resources you've already used, the amount of nodes requested and the priority/size of other projects running.
